I know I am not alone in this, but I've been dealing with an itch to write. Shocker, I know. And I hear you- 'Well, sounds like a good time to get onto that WIP, or the other one, or that other one'- and you aren't *wrong*. But something about the words that were itching at my fingertips, asking for me to put pen to paper, I knew they didn't belong with my current WIPs.

Not to mention I hadn't brought any of them with me, but that's neither here nor there. {class="small"}

And I hear you again- 'Ah, so you started a new project I bet. Classic' - but no, I resisted that habit for once.

Instead of trying to plan out some new project that would probably be fun for like an hour and then sit in my 'I Swear Its Not Abandoned' pile, I pulled out an old favorite: Story Prompts. How did that not end with a new WIP? By allowing myself to write fragments.

I know, I know, fragments of anything are a frowned upon around writing circles, but think of it more like a sketch. Or a doodle. Actually I like doodle better. Low pressure, just for fun, small. Perfect. Personally I like the ones that give you a first sentence or first part of a sentence to start with, makes it easier to riff from there but without needing to keep anything else in mind. Just let the words flow and show me where they want to go. Like looking at a scribble and seeing what shapes jump out.

Shall I share a few? I present them as I originally wrote them, as much as I already see edits I would like to make.

***

> \#2: "He had the urge to clear the ground, to look out and see nothing."

He had the urge to clear the ground, to look out and see nothing. It was impossible, of course, no matter where you looked there was always *something*.

"Unless its a vacuum, I suppose," he thought to himself.

So perhaps nothing was not achievable, no, but the idea of blankness stuck with him. This had its own appeal. Blankness held eternal possibility, and eternal regret. The infinity of nothing and everything wrapped into something a mortal mind could comprehend.

Blankness

This, he could work with. And so turned from the damnation of his tower window and back to the depths of the study. But how to create it? He considered the most obvious answer- lest it plague his mind later- but the blankness of unwritten paper was only a faux one. A lie, in the truth of scrutiny. Each leaf closer to its wild namesake than that which he sought. Even under the light of the naked eye and lantern light, the man could see the grain and fiber and fluff that had made each piece. Notable in each unique piece, but unsuitable for his purposes.

The open sky was dismissed even quicker for the nature of air made it *something*, also gave it a movement that was most certainly never **blank**.

Perhaps it was a fool's game to look upon the external in this quest.

Perhaps true stillness could only be achieved in the mind's theater.

And so it was.

> \#3: "The tunnel was his only way out..."

The tunnel was his only way out, so *of course *it was collapsed midway.

Mostly collapsed.

*\~Nearly\~ completely.*

Functionally useless. Useless to everyone but him. Honestly his luck couldn't be better. A quick shift into something small and mole-like and he was on his way again.

> \#4: "She decided to go to her father's grave, to ask his advice."

She decided to go to her father's grave, to ask his advice. Of course he wasn't there, but a quick prayer and a flower on his headstone, and she had a good idea where to start.

He was refreshingly straight-forward that night- a clear image of a particular park bench he'd favored in life. Still, she kept an eye out along the way. He was wont to put things in her path he thought she should know as often as find her a destination.

Sure enough, one of the corner markets had her favorite energy drink on clearance sale. She made a mental note to borrow Keith's car tomorrow and stock up.

> \#5: "The horse came back alone..."

The horse came back alone, again.

Perhaps next time would go better.

***

Hopefully you can see the sketch or doodle metaphor a little better? Each one *suggests *a world of possibly, but *commits *to little. And so many different vibes. Almost like writing 5 different stories in like an hour.

(Where is #1? I didn't like it as much as the others.) {class="small"}

So, if you're a writer type and looking for a writing equivalent of a doodle, give this a thought and a shot.
